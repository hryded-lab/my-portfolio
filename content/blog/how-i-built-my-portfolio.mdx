# I Built This Portfolio by Vibe Coding — Here's What I Actually Learned

I'm a Manufacturing Engineering student. Not a CS student. I don't have semesters of algorithms and data structures behind me — I have curiosity, a laptop, and a genuine interest in building things.

So when I decided I needed a portfolio, I didn't do it the traditional way. I built it by vibe coding — using AI (Claude, mostly) to write large chunks of the code while I directed, reviewed, debugged, and shaped it into something I actually wanted.

Here's what that process taught me.

## What Vibe Coding Actually Means

It's not "let AI do everything and take credit." That produces garbage. It's closer to being an architect who can't lay every brick themselves — you need to understand the structure, make the decisions, and know when something is wrong even if you couldn't have built it from scratch.

In practice, that meant: I knew I wanted a Windows XP desktop aesthetic. I knew I wanted a Clippy-inspired AI assistant. I knew I wanted a command prompt with easter eggs. Turning those ideas into working code required understanding what I was asking for, reviewing the output critically, catching bugs, and making judgment calls constantly.

## What You Learn by Prompting

This surprised me: the process of prompting AI well forces you to think more precisely than you might otherwise.

To get a good result, you have to describe what you want clearly enough that an AI can implement it. That means understanding the problem, the constraints, the edge cases. You can't be vague and get something good back. The feedback loop sharpens your thinking.

I learned more about React hooks, state management, and streaming APIs by trying to get them right through prompting than I would have by passively reading documentation.

## What Still Required Me

- Every design decision. AI doesn't know what looks right for your personality.
- Debugging. When something breaks in a subtle way, you need to understand the system well enough to diagnose it — AI can help, but you have to lead the investigation.
- Content. The words, the descriptions, the voice — those are yours. No prompt fixes shallow content.
- Taste. Knowing when something is good enough versus when it needs more work. That's not something you can delegate.

## The Honest Part

I started this project to get away from the boredom of the semester, make something cool to show for future opportunities, and genuinely learn how to work with AI tools effectively. I didn't expect it to become as involved as it did.

The portfolio ended up with a full boot sequence, a lock screen, draggable windows, a streaming AI assistant, a functional command prompt, and more. Every feature came from a conversation — me directing, AI implementing, me revising.

Whether that counts as "building" is a question worth sitting with. My answer: yes. The ideas, the decisions, the judgment calls — those were mine. The AI was a fast pair programmer who could write code I'd have taken much longer to write alone.

## What I'd Tell Someone Thinking About Doing This

Start with a clear vision of what you want to build. The clearer your idea, the better the AI can help you realize it. If you're vague, you'll get something generic.

Review everything. Don't ship code you don't understand. Use the AI's output as a starting point, not a final answer.

And build something you actually want to exist — not just a template with your name on it. That's what will make you push through the frustrating parts.
